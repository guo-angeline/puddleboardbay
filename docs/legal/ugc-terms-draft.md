# UGC Contributor Terms: Draft for Attorney Review

**Status:** First-draft, risk-reduction document produced by the studio `lawyer` agent (D24 Q1, owner answered "yes, engage an attorney"). It exists to make the licensed-attorney engagement a redline/markup job rather than a from-blank draft. It is **not legal advice, not final, and not for publication.** Every `[ATTORNEY: ...]` bracket marks a judgment call reserved for that attorney. Nothing here ships until the attorney has reviewed it and the owner accepts it.

**Scope: NATIONWIDE UNITED STATES.** Revised July 21, 2026. The owner remains a sole operator based in San Francisco, California, but Paddle to Water is expanding beyond the Bay Area to launch spots across the United States. Draft for a **50-state consumer audience** from the start, not for California with a later retrofit. Two consequences run through the whole document: (a) contributors will be residents of any state, so state consumer-protection, privacy, assent, and forum-selection rules all vary; and (b) reviews will name **private businesses located in many different states** (marinas, paddle shops, private docks), so defamation exposure and anti-SLAPP protection vary by where the reviewed business sits and where it can sue. California is still the presumptive governing law because the operator is here, but that choice is now a contested question rather than a default, see Section 12.

**Baked-in decisions (D24, unchanged):** Q2 = required sign-in (Google), so every contributor is an authenticated 18+ account holder. Q3 = binary publish-or-reject pre-moderation queue, email-on-submit to the sole moderator (owner), no auto-publish ever, no in-place editing of user text (a Section 230 constraint), launched to all spots.

**Engagement note for the owner:** a California attorney can and should own this document. Several questions in Part 2 turn on the law of states where the attorney is not licensed. The right ask is not "become a 50-state expert," it is "tell me which of these need local counsel in a specific state, and which can be handled by drafting defensively so no single state's rule breaks the contract."

My understanding of the general framework is that forum-selection clauses in consumer contracts are commonly enforced under the federal standard absent fraud, overreaching, or a showing that the forum is gravely inconvenient, and that a choice-of-law clause is generally honored unless the chosen state has no substantial relationship to the parties (California plainly does here) **or** applying the chosen law would contravene a fundamental public policy of a state with a materially greater interest, which is typically the consumer's home state. Please verify that framework and, more usefully, identify whether any states in scope **restrict by statute** forum-selection or choice-of-law clauses against their own residents in consumer contracts, and whether any state consumer-protection statutes in scope are **non-waivable regardless of a choice-of-law clause**. I am not confident of the specific state list and am deliberately not naming one.

**(b) The practical read the owner needs.** Even if the clause is largely enforceable, it is worth telling the owner plainly what it does and does not buy. It gives real leverage in a dispute with a **contributor**. It does **nothing** against a reviewed business suing over a hosted review, because that business never agreed to it (see 5.2(e)). Since the reviewed-business suit is the more likely and more expensive scenario, please make sure the owner does not over-value this clause.

**(c) Interaction with Section 10.4.** If the choice-of-law clause fails against a resident of a state hostile to liability caps or pre-injury releases, that state's law may govern the enforceability of the cap. That dependency is why 10.4(c) asks for a savings construction rather than a single California-optimized cap. Please make the two clauses consistent with each other.

**(d) Small-claims carve-out.** Advise whether to preserve a small-claims option in the consumer's home jurisdiction, which is common practice, costs the Site almost nothing, and materially improves how a court reads the fairness of the whole dispute-resolution package.]

12.2 **Arbitration and class waiver.** [ATTORNEY: decide whether to include a binding arbitration clause and a class-action waiver, and if so draft them to current enforceability standards. **The nationwide audience changes the calculus in both directions, so please weigh it explicitly rather than defaulting either way:**

**Argument for.** The Federal Arbitration Act is federal and broadly preempts state-law hostility to arbitration, so a properly drafted arbitration clause with a class waiver is the **most uniform** dispute-resolution mechanism available across 50 states. It arguably does the job the Section 12.1 venue clause is trying to do, more reliably, because it does not depend on each state's willingness to honor a forum-selection clause against its residents.

**Argument against.** Mass-arbitration cost exposure is real and asymmetric for a solo operator with no revenue: administrative and arbitrator fees are typically borne largely by the business, and a coordinated filing can cost more than the underlying claims. There is no plausible class-action target here today (a free site, no money changing hands, no consumer-financial harm pattern), which is the usual reason to want a class waiver in the first place.

**Please also address:** the assent and conspicuousness standard needed for an arbitration clause to bind a consumer, noting that at least some states require notably clearer waiver-of-rights language than others (I believe New Jersey is the standard example, please verify); whether an opt-out window should be offered; the arbitration provider and fee-allocation terms; and whether the class waiver survives if the arbitration clause is struck. If the recommendation is no arbitration, say so plainly, since a solo operator adding an arbitration clause "because everyone has one" may be buying mass-arbitration exposure to solve a problem it does not have.]

12.3 [ATTORNEY: if arbitration is not used, consider a jury-trial waiver and a limitations-shortening clause. Nationwide note: pre-dispute contractual jury-trial waivers are **not** enforceable in every state (some states hold them invalid or require particular procedures), and the enforceability of a clause shortening a statutory limitations period also varies by state and by claim type. Please advise which of these are worth including given that variation, or whether they are better dropped than drafted unenforceably.]

12.4 **Savings and severability across jurisdictions.** [ATTORNEY: please draft a savings and severability construction fit for a nationwide consumer contract, so that (a) if any clause, in particular the liability cap in 10.4, the indemnity in Section 8, or any arbitration or jury-waiver clause, is unenforceable under the law of a given state, it is automatically limited to the maximum extent that state permits rather than struck in whole, and (b) the invalidity of one clause as to one user in one state does not invalidate the rest of the agreement or the same clause as to other users. This construction is the main defensive tool for a single document serving 50 states, and I want your drafting on it rather than boilerplate. Please also confirm whether any state treats an aggressive savings clause itself as evidence of overreach.]
